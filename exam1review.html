Unit 1: Foundations of Life

What is Homeostasis?

+

Definition: Homeostasis is the maintenance of a relatively stable internal environment despite continuous outside changes.

It is a dynamic state of equilibrium where conditions vary but always within narrow limits.

What are Feedback Loops?

+

Most Common: Negative Feedback (e.g., Body temperature, blood sugar). It shuts off the original stimulus or reduces its intensity.

Dangerous/Special: Positive Feedback (e.g., Blood clotting, childbirth). It enhances the original stimulus so the response is accelerated.

Atom Components: Protons, Neutrons, Electrons

+
  • Protons (+): Positive charge, located in the nucleus. Number of protons determines the element.
  • Neutrons (0): Neutral charge, located in the nucleus.
  • Electrons (-): Negative charge, orbit the nucleus in shells.

Isotopes vs. Ions

+

Isotopes: Atoms of the same element that differ in the number of neutrons. They have the same atomic number but different mass numbers.

Ions: Atoms that have gained or lost electrons, resulting in a positive or negative charge.

Why do chemical bonds occur?

+

Chemical bonds occur to fill the valence shell (outermost shell) with 8 electrons (the Octet Rule), reaching a state of maximum stability.

Polarity & Solubility

+

Polar Molecules: Unequal sharing of electrons (e.g., Water). They have partial charges.

Nonpolar Molecules: Equal sharing of electrons (e.g., Fats/Lipids).

"Like dissolves like" - Polar dissolves in polar; nonpolar dissolves in nonpolar.

Acids vs. Bases

+

Acids: Proton donors. They release $H^+$ in solution (pH < 7).

Bases: Proton acceptors. They take up $H^+$ or release $OH^-$ (pH > 7).

Unit 2: Cell Biology & Central Dogma

The Plasma Membrane Structure

+

Known as the Fluid Mosaic Model. Composed of a double layer of Phospholipids with embedded proteins, cholesterol, and carbohydrates.

Selective Permeability

+

The membrane allows some substances to pass while excluding others. Small, nonpolar molecules (like $O_2$ or $CO_2$) pass easily through the lipid core.

Organelle Functions (Quick Reference)

+

Nucleus:

Store DNA / Control Center

Mitochondria:

Powerhouse / Produce ATP

Ribosomes:

Protein synthesis site

Rough ER:

Modifies & packs proteins

Central Dogma: From DNA to Protein

+

1. Transcription (Nucleus): DNA is used as a template to make mRNA.

2. Translation (Cytoplasm/Ribosome): mRNA is decoded by tRNA to build a chain of amino acids (protein).

Cell Cycle & Mitosis

+

Mitosis: Nuclear division (Prophase, Metaphase, Anaphase, Telophase).

Interphase: Where the cell spends most of its life (G1, S, G2 phases).

Note: Mitosis is actually a very short part of the total cell cycle!

Unit 3: Communication & Enzymes

Cell Communication Types

+

Autocrine: Cell signals itself.

Paracrine: Cell signals immediate neighbors.

Endocrine: Cell signals distant organs via the bloodstream (Hormones).

Enzymes & Catalysis

+

Enzymes are biological catalysts (usually proteins). They speed up reactions by lowering the activation energy required for a reaction to begin.

Tissue Repair Steps

+
  1. Inflammation: Prepare the site for repair.
  2. Proliferation: New tissue formation.
  3. Remodeling: Mature the new tissue.

Question 1

Define Homeostasis and Feedback Loops.

+

Answer: Homeostasis is the ability to maintain stable internal conditions despite environmental changes. It involves feedback loops which are monitoring systems.

Types: Negative feedback loops work to reverse a change to bring body back to set point (common). Positive feedback loops work to intensify a change until an endpoint is reached (rare/clotting).

Question 2

Response to Hot Temperatures.

+

The 5 Components:

  • Stimulus: High external/internal heat.
  • Receptor: Thermoreceptors (Skin/Blood).
  • Control Center: Hypothalamus (Brain).
  • Effector: Sweat glands / Blood vessels.
  • Response: Sweating / Vasodilation (to cool down).
Question 3

The Importance of Valence Shells.

+

The valence shell is the outermost shell of an atom. Atoms are stable only when this shell is full (usually 8 electrons).

Link to Bonds: If the shell is nearly full, the atom might steal electrons (Ionic). If it's half-full, it might share (Covalent). If it's full (Noble Gases), it won't bond at all.

Question 4

Protein Synthesis Walkthrough.

+

Phase 1: Nucleus (Transcription)

DNA acts as a master blueprint. RNA Polymerase reads DNA and creates an mRNA strand.

Phase 2: Cytoplasm (Translation)

mRNA exits the nucleus and attaches to a Ribosome. tRNA molecules bring amino acids that match the mRNA code to build the protein chain.
